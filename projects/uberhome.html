UberHOME

Conceptual UI/UX Design Case Study

Deliver IKEA furniture straight to your new home with the help of Uber’s partners.

I completed this case study in third-year business class with a team of five other students. The goal was to design a hypothetical partnership for Uber. While we all made contributions to every part of the case, I was in charge of the UI/UX section, created all the vector graphics, and designed all of the wireframes.

Overview.

/01

The Problem

As a student living alone in a brand new city, I rented a shared apartment to save money and walking to class was my daily commute.

Like many others, I needed to shop online for furniture. Just the basics: a bed frame, a desk, and a dresser. But without a car, I had no way of transporting the furniture back home and shipping from the nearest store was very expensive given how sizable each package could be.

/02

Our Solution

We designed UberHOME to alleviate this problem by harnessing the benefits of modern rideshare to bring affordable furniture to students and young adults.

The demand for low-cost and well-designed furniture is steadily growing likely from increasing immigration in Canada. IKEA has the greatest market share in the furniture industry and offers affordable prices and attractive designs. However, their physical stores are too far for students who do not own cars and shipping is very costly.

In a unique partnership between Uber and IKEA, UberHOME offers online shopping at IKEA and delivery of furniture orders through Uber's drivers.

IKEA Analysis

As immigration and international residents increase, it follows that demand for housing and furnishing increase as well. Market-dominant distributors like IKEA can benefit greatly from this trend as they are able to offer new home-owners and/or renters basic, cheap furniture products. Students and young adults with limited disposable income depend on these affordable furniture options. We discovered many of these customers have considered purchasing from IKEA but did not follow through because they lacked a method of transporting the furniture from the nearest store/pick-up center to their homes.

IKEA is well-known for their beautifully decorated showrooms which also serves as a marketing tool for customers visiting their brick-and-mortar locations. However, when people find an item they like when browsing the showrooms but it is not in stock at that location, the only two solutions are to ship the item (expensive and takes time) or go to a different location where it is in stock (inconvenient).


Online shopping and flexible delivery options can solve both these problems.

‍Over the past decade, online shopping has become an integral part of society (Euromonitor, 2018) and has been a major time-saver for many. For people who don’t have a car, or who have busy schedules, online shopping is both an accessible and efficient alternative.

Shipping fees for all items bought on IKEA’s online website starts at a $59 for larger furniture like bed frames, $39 for smaller furnitures. and $9.99 for small decorative pieces and accessories. Similar to most shipping options, this price will vary based on weight and distance, and the delivery process can usually take over a week even for the smallest items. Even curbside pick-up options will cost a minimum of $5 per order.

Uber Analysis

This is where Uber can help.

The shipping industry is dominated by key companies which could make it difficult to negotiate lower prices. Uber, with access to millions of driver partners, has the resources to disrupt it by facilitating on-demand shipping services. Ride-hailing is one of the fastest growing industries in the modern world and Uber already holds nearly 70% of the market share (Statista, 2021).

This is a great opportunity for people who were previously hesitant to partner with Uber because they don’t want a stranger riding in their car. Picking up furniture from IKEA stores and delivering them would only require only a moderately sized vehicle and some time.

/02

Value Proposition

UberHOME offers the accessibility and flexibility of modern rideshare to deliver affordable furniture to students and young adults.

The platform would provide a simple online shopping interface where users can browse available products at their selected nearest IKEA store. Exclusive deals only available to UberHOME can help attract new users to shop on the platform. Users will also be able to place new orders or add existing IKEA orders for pick-up and delivery by one of Uber’s qualifying driver partners. Coordination of delivery and drop-off can be facilitated through the app’s live tracking, similar to existing Uber features.

Design.

/01

Branding

Loyal Uber customers would be more willing to try a new Uber partnerships, and will likely make up most of the early adopters for our service. With this in mind we incorporated many of Uber’s existing branding and elements into UberHOME for a smoother adoption process (ride-hailing, real-time tracking, review system). Many interface elements will already be familiar to them and thus easier to understand.

However, to avoid too much brand dilution, UberHOME needs be distant enough in stylistic elements for the service to be recognized as a separate entity. I chose bright blue colors to accent the traditional Uber black, and the name is in the same font as all Uber subsidiaries. I created a new slogan - YOUR HOME, YOUR MOVE - to highlight the key features of the app (moving furniture) and a call to action for consumers to “make the move”.

Colour Palette & Typography
‍
I chose Helvetica because of its simplistic, minimal design which fits the modern look of the app. The black colour is highly prominent in Uber’s products and we complemented it with shades of blue.

Logo
‍
To help design our logo, I studied the logo of Uber’s established subsidiary, UberEats, and noted its similarities with Uber’s original logo. Sticking with the same formula for consistency, I used the same font for the “Uber” and added “Home” to the end in blue.

Reflections.

Final Thoughts

"Build a partnership opportunity for Uber" is a very generic prompt with many paths we could've taken. We had to choose a specific direction to pay attention to (immigration, housing, furniture) and I think we did a good job of targeting the needs of the people around us - our classmates and friends - to build our solution around. I believe this laid a good foundation for our success.

It was also much more manageable to study a smaller target group that was closer to us, than something like the general population of North America. We were able to interview people in the sample to make sense of their problem and build connections between the pieces of the puzzle.

Overall, I believe business and design are in some ways very closely interconnected. Design-thinking and user-centered design practices can also be applied to business problems to develop solutions that customers can get behind. However, this isn't a rule but more of a guideline as I know every case is different. As a famous businessman once said:

“If I had asked people what they wanted, they would have said faster horses.”

Henry Ford

Personally, I believe what is more important than centering solutions around what users need or innovating products that users don't yet know they need is to involve the user throughout the entire process to keep our cars and horses on the right path. You can quote me on that.
